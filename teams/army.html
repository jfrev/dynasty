Week 8

No. 19 and Idle: Army Uses the Bye to Let a 6-1 Start Sink In

A first-year coach with a complicated history has the Black Knights ranked for the first time in years. Nobody in the building is calling it a surprise.

By Colonel Pete Renner (Ret.) · The Long Gray Line Dispatch

Army's first ranking of the season dropped this week at a convenient time — the middle of a bye — giving a locker room that doesn't usually get much outside attention a week to actually enjoy it. At 6-1 and 4-1 in a brand-new Big 12 home, No. 19 is the highest the program has been ranked in years.

Da'Rock has stayed characteristically unbothered by the attention. His message in meetings this week, according to players, has been about tape review and depth chart maintenance, not the poll. The option offense has been as advertised: patient, physical, and murder on a defense's fourth-quarter conditioning.

The schedule doesn't get easier after the bye, and everyone here knows a ranking is worth exactly nothing in November if it isn't backed up. For now, though, a program that's used to being overlooked gets a week to sit with being looked at.

The Option Doesn't Care What You Watched On Film — And Neither Does Its New Head Coach

Pound Da'Rock arrives at West Point with a title résumé from Idaho and a complicated history with the uniform his players wear.

By Colonel Pete Renner (Ret.) · The Long Gray Line Dispatch

Every year, some defensive coordinator says the same thing into a microphone: we know exactly what Army is going to do, we just have to execute our assignments. Every year, Army finds the one soft edge in that execution and rides it forty yards. This year, the man drawing up the answers is new to the building — Pound Da'Rock, whose own history with the Army is complicated, but whose coaching record at Idaho speaks for itself.

Da'Rock inherits the most experienced option backfield West Point has had in years. The offensive line, undersized by nearly every other program's standards, is once again betting on leverage and technique over size.

Defensively, discipline is the whole identity: gang tackle, don't chase highlights, make the other team execute an eighteen-play drive if they want to score.
